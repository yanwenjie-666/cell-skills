## Punctuation Rules

### Serial (Oxford) Comma — REQUIRED
- ✅ "RNA, DNA, and protein"
- ❌ "RNA, DNA and protein"

### Em Dash
- Use em dash (—) with no surrounding spaces for parenthetical statements
- "The protein—which is highly conserved—plays a critical role..."

### Hyphens
- Compound modifiers before nouns: "well-characterized pathway," "single-cell analysis"
- No hyphen after -ly adverbs: "newly identified gene" (not "newly-identified")
- Prefixes generally no hyphen: nonspecific, coexpression, pretreatment, reanalysis
- Exceptions: re-examine (to avoid confusion), co-immunoprecipitation

### Semicolons
- Use between closely related independent clauses
- Use in complex lists where items contain commas

## Numbers and Units

### Numerals vs Words
- Spell out: one through nine (unless with units)
- Use numerals: 10 and above
- Always numerals: with units (5 μL, 3 days), percentages (5%), ratios (2:1)
- At sentence start: always spell out ("Thirty mice were used...")

### Units and Spacing
- Space between number and unit: 10 μM, 37°C, 5 min, 24 h
- No space: 10% (percentage), 5× (fold)
- Time: use "h" (hours), "min" (minutes), "s" (seconds) — NOT "hr" or "sec"
- Concentrations: 10 mM, 5 μg/mL (not μg/ml)
- Temperature: 37°C (degree sign attached to C, space after number)

### Scientific Notation
- 3 × 10⁵ (not 3E5 or 3*10^5 in text)
- Multiplication sign (×), not letter x

## Gene and Protein Nomenclature

### Human Genes/Proteins
- Gene: *TP53*, *BRCA1* (italic, all caps)
- Protein: TP53, BRCA1 (roman, all caps)

### Mouse Genes/Proteins
- Gene: *Tp53*, *Brca1* (italic, initial cap)
- Protein: TP53, BRCA1 (roman, all caps — same as human)

### Drosophila
- Gene: *white*, *hedgehog* (italic, lowercase)
- Protein: White, Hedgehog (roman, initial cap)

### General Rules
- Alleles: superscript or described in text
- Transgenes: italicized
- Loci: roman, with chromosome notation

## Section-Specific Style Notes

### Summary Style
- Self-contained, no citations
- Context (1–2 sentences) → Gap/Question (1 sentence) → Approach (1–2 sentences) → Key findings (2–4 sentences) → Significance (1 sentence)
- No jargon without definition
- No abbreviations (except DNA, RNA, etc.)
- ≤150 words strictly

### Introduction Style
- Broad context → narrowing focus → knowledge gap → "Here, we..."
- Typically ends with: "Here, we show/demonstrate/report that [main finding], revealing/identifying [significance]."
- 3–5 paragraphs, not exhaustive literature review
- Cite selectively (recent, relevant, authoritative)

### Results Subheadings
- **Declarative**: State the conclusion, not the method
- ✅ "CDK4 Phosphorylation Is Required for G1-S Transition"
- ❌ "Analysis of CDK4 Phosphorylation"
- ❌ "Results of CDK4 Experiments"
- Capitalize major words (title case)

### Discussion Style
- Paragraph 1: Brief summary of key findings (2–3 sentences)
- Body: Implications, mechanisms, comparisons to literature
- Limitations: Acknowledged honestly, not defensively
- Future directions: Specific, testable predictions
- Model paragraph: "We propose a model in which..."
- Final paragraph: Broader significance

## Transition Phrases (Cell Press Favorites)

### Introducing Results
- "We first sought to determine..."
- "To address this question, we..."
- "We reasoned that if X were true, then Y..."

### Building on Previous Results
- "Given that X caused Y (Figure 1), we next asked..."
- "Having established that..., we investigated..."
- "Prompted by these observations, we..."
- "Building on these findings, we..."

### Presenting Unexpected Results
- "Strikingly, we found that..."
- "Unexpectedly, our analysis revealed..."
- "Surprisingly, X had no effect on..."
- "In contrast to our prediction,..."

### Drawing Conclusions
- "These data demonstrate that..."
- "Together, these results indicate..."
- "Taken together, our findings suggest a model in which..."
- "Collectively, these data support..."

### Contrasting
- "In contrast to..."
- "Unlike X, Y showed..."
- "Although X was true, Y was not..."
- "While previous studies suggested X, our data indicate Y..."

## Formatting Details

### Emphasis
- Use italics for emphasis (sparingly)
- Use bold only for defined terms at first introduction (rare in Cell)
- NEVER use all caps for emphasis in text

### Lists
- Cell Press papers rarely use bullet lists in main text
- Exceptions: Key Resources Table, STAR Methods subheadings
- Prefer narrative prose in Results and Discussion

### Abbreviations
- Define at first use: "chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP)"
- Re-define in each major section if helpful
- Do not define in title
- Limit total abbreviations — if used fewer than 3 times, spell out each time

## Common Errors in Cell Submissions

1. Using "significant" without statistical context
2. Overusing hedge words (might, possibly, could)
3. Passive voice throughout (acceptable occasionally, not as default)
4. Describing methods in Results (should be in STAR Methods)
5. Conclusions in Results that belong in Discussion
6. Over-citing in Introduction (keep it focused)
7. Identical sentences in Summary and Introduction
8. Missing figure references for key claims
